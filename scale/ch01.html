The Hug of Death

from “millions of users” to a number you can actually test

In this chapter you'll learn ✍️

  • how to turn “millions of users” into requests per second
  • why reads vastly outnumber writes (and why that's good news)
  • throughput vs latency, and why averages lie — meet p50/p95/p99
  • the measurement loop the whole book runs on

Your post hits the front page

You built gopherit in the last book. It works beautifully for you and a few friends. Then someone posts a link to it, it climbs to #1, and a crowd arrives all at once — the hug of death. Does your server hug back, or fall over?

The honest answer, which we'll prove in the next chapter: as written, gopherit falls over at about 3 requests per second. Not 3 thousand. Three. But by the last page it handles tens of thousands — on the same laptop, without a single new machine. The gap between those two numbers is this whole book, and it's all measurement.

Millions of users ≠ millions of requests

“Can it handle a million users?” is the wrong question, because users aren't requests. Let's do the napkin math. Say a million daily active users, each making ~50 requests a day (scrolling, voting, the odd post):

The napkin

1,000,000 users × 50 req/day = 50,000,000 req/day
÷ 86,400 seconds/day ≈ ~580 req/s average
× ~5 (traffic isn't flat — evenings spike) ≈ ~3,000 req/s peak

So a million daily users is a few thousand requests per second at peak. That's a number you can hold in your head — and, spoiler, a number one well-tuned Go box can serve.

And here's the kindest fact in all of backend engineering: the traffic is overwhelmingly reads. On a Reddit-shaped site, well over 90% of requests are “show me the feed.” People lurk far more than they post. Every read is cacheable, shardable, and cheap to scale — which is why chapters 4 and 5 (the read path) buy us the biggest wins by far.

Averages lie — meet the percentiles

Two numbers describe a system under load. Throughput is how many requests per second you serve. Latency is how long each one takes. And you must never summarise latency with an average.

Picture a café. Nine customers are served in 1 minute; one gets stuck behind a broken card reader for 10 minutes. The average wait is ~2 minutes — a number literally nobody experienced. The truth is “most people wait 1 minute, but 1 in 10 waits 10.” That's what percentiles tell you:

PercentileReads asWhy you care
p50 (median)half of requests are faster than thisthe typical experience
p9595% are faster; 1 in 20 is sloweryour unlucky-but-common users
p9999% are faster; 1 in 100 is slowerthe tail that fills your support inbox
The tail is the story

At scale, the p99 is the experience — with a million users, “1 in 100” is ten thousand angry people. A system with a great average and a terrible p99 is a system that's quietly on fire. Our load tester reports p50/p95/p99, never just an average, and so should you.

The loop we'll run eleven times

Every fix in this book follows the same discipline. Never optimise by vibes; optimise by evidence:

load it→ watch it break→ profile→ fix one thing→ measure again↺

The “profile” step is the one people skip, and it's the most important. In chapter 4 we'll reach for the fix everyone reaches for first — a bigger database connection pool — and watch it take us from a miserable 3 req/s to a still-miserable 6. The real culprit was hiding in the query plan, somewhere we'd never have guessed. Guessing is how you spend a week optimising the wrong thing.

Pin to your brain 📌

  • A million daily users ≈ a few thousand req/s at peak — a testable number.
  • ~95% of traffic is reads: cacheable, cheap, the biggest wins live here.
  • Summarise latency with p50/p95/p99, never the average. The tail is the experience.
  • Measure → break → profile → fix one thing → measure again. Never guess.
